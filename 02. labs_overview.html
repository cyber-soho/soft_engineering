Exercise 1: Applying Foundational Principles 60 minutes

Scenario: A university professor wants to commission a simple “Automated Grader” application for their multiple‑choice exams. The application should be able to:

  • Scan a standardized answer sheet (e.g., a Scantron form image).
  • Compare the scanned answers against a correct answer key provided by the professor.
  • Calculate a final score for each student.
  • Export the results (Student Name, Student ID, Score) into a single CSV file.

Task: Using the problem‑solving framework and the guiding principles discussed in the lecture, describe how you would approach the initial stages of this project. Specifically, structure your answer to address the following four points, integrating at least three of Hooker’s seven principles where you see fit.

  1. Understand the Problem: Who are the stakeholders? What are the key objectives and potential unknowns?
  2. Plan a Solution: How would you model a solution? Which principles would guide your high‑level design choices?
  3. Carry Out the Plan: From a development perspective, what considerations are important for ensuring the code is maintainable and understandable in the future?
  4. Examine the Result: How would you and the professor verify that the application works correctly and delivers value?
Helpful prompts (optional)
  • Map your points to Polya’s steps and explicitly name at least three of Hooker’s principles (e.g., “The Reason It All Exists”, “KISS”, “Be Open to the Future”, “What You Produce, Others Will Consume”).
  • Consider risks: image quality, ambiguous markings, misaligned sheets, different file formats.
  • Think verification: golden datasets, unit/integration tests, usability feedback, and CSV validation.

Exercise 2: Selecting the Right Process Model 60 minutes

Scenario: You are a software project manager who must recommend a development process model for three different upcoming projects.

  • Project A: The Avionics Update. You are developing safety‑critical software for an airplane’s navigation system. The requirements are fully defined by federal aviation regulations, are non‑negotiable, and will not change. Every step of the process must be meticulously documented and verifiable for a safety audit.
  • Project B: The Food Discovery App. A startup wants to build a new mobile app that gives users personalized restaurant recommendations. They have a basic vision but are unsure which features users will find most engaging (e.g., social sharing, chef interviews, dietary filtering). The market is competitive, and the ability to get user feedback early and adapt quickly is crucial.
  • Project C: The Corporate Intranet Portal. You manage the IT team for a large company. Your team handles a continuous stream of requests to update the company’s internal website. These requests are varied—from fixing a broken link to adding a new page for the HR department or updating the company calendar. The priority of these tasks can change daily based on management’s needs.

Task: For each project (A, B, and C), perform the following:

  1. Select a Process Model: Choose the most appropriate software process model from the lecture (e.g., Waterfall, Prototyping, Spiral, Scrum, Kanban).
  2. Justify Your Choice: Explain why your chosen model is a good fit. Connect the project’s characteristics (e.g., stability of requirements, level of risk, need for feedback) to the strengths of the model.
  3. Describe the First Step: Briefly outline what the very first activity or phase would look like for that project under your chosen model.
